# Persuasion Principles — 7 Cialdini Principles

Robert Cialdini identified 7 universal principles of persuasion. Each principle describes a psychological pattern that drives human compliance. In advertising, these principles inform how to structure the message, the offer, and the social context around the product.

**Usage rule:** A single ad can leverage 1-2 principles. More than that feels like a sales trap. Choose based on awareness stage and persona's dominant objection.

---

## The 7 Principles

### P1 — Reciprocity
**Mechanism:** When someone gives us something, we feel an automatic obligation to give back. The gift creates a debt.
**Ad application:** Lead with genuine value before asking for anything. Free guides, templates, tips, audits — the prospect feels they owe you their attention or their click.
**Example:** "Here's the exact 5-step framework we use for €50k/month Meta accounts. Free." → CTA: "Get the full system"
**When to use:** Unaware → Solution Aware (TOF/MOF). Education-first content.
**Common mistake:** Giving something worthless and expecting reciprocity. The gift must have real perceived value.

---

### P2 — Commitment & Consistency
**Mechanism:** Once people commit to something (even a small action), they feel compelled to act consistently with that commitment.
**Ad application:** Get a micro-commitment first — a quiz answer, a free trial signup, a "yes" to a simple question. Each step makes the next step easier.
**Example:** Hook: "Do you spend more than €1,000/month on ads?" → If yes, you're now self-identified as the target → next steps feel consistent.
**When to use:** Problem Aware → Product Aware. Funnel sequences, multi-touch campaigns.
**Common mistake:** Asking for too big a commitment too early. Start small: click → email → trial → purchase.

---

### P3 — Social Proof
**Mechanism:** When uncertain, people look to others — especially similar others — to determine correct behavior.
**Ad application:** Show volume ("12,000 users"), similarity ("entrepreneurs like you"), and specificity ("Sarah went from €2k to €47k/month in 90 days").
**Example:** "2,400 Creative Strategists already use this workflow. Here's why." + testimonial montage.
**When to use:** Solution Aware → Most Aware (MOF/BOF). When the prospect is choosing between options.
**Common mistake:** Vague social proof. "Many customers love us" = zero credibility. Specific numbers, names, and results only.

---

### P4 — Authority
**Mechanism:** People defer to experts and credentialed figures, especially in domains where they feel uncertain.
**Ad application:** Establish expertise in the first 3-5 seconds. Credentials, results managed, media mentions, years of experience. The authority must be relevant to the claim.
**Example:** "After managing €12M in Meta ad spend across 200+ brands — here's the #1 mistake I see."
**When to use:** Solution Aware → Product Aware (MOF). When credibility is the bottleneck to trust.
**Common mistake:** Claiming authority without proof. "I'm an expert" means nothing. "I managed €12M in ad spend" means everything.

---

### P5 — Liking
**Mechanism:** People say yes to people they like. Liking is driven by similarity, compliments, familiarity, and cooperation.
**Ad application:** Use relatable creators, show the founder's real personality, mirror the prospect's language and values. UGC works because the creator feels "like me."
**Example:** Founder video: "I was exactly where you are 3 years ago. Burning budget, no system, no results. Here's what changed."
**When to use:** Unaware → Problem Aware (TOF). Brand-building, trust-building, first-touch content.
**Common mistake:** Using polished actors when the persona values authenticity. Match the messenger to the audience.

---

### P6 — Scarcity
**Mechanism:** Limited availability increases perceived value. What's scarce feels more desirable, and the fear of missing out drives action.
**Ad application:** Genuine time limits, stock limits, cohort sizes, or access windows. The scarcity must be real or implicitly justifiable.
**Example:** "April cohort: 6 spots remaining. Next opening is September." + countdown.
**When to use:** Product Aware → Most Aware (BOF). Retargeting, cart abandonment, launch sequences.
**Common mistake:** Fake scarcity. "Only 3 left!" on a digital product with unlimited inventory. The audience knows. Trust is destroyed.

---

### P7 — Unity
**Mechanism:** People are more influenced by those they perceive as part of their in-group — shared identity, not just shared opinions.
**Ad application:** Position the brand as part of the prospect's tribe. "We" language, shared enemies, shared values, community membership.
**Example:** "We're the Creative Strategists who refuse to run ads without a hypothesis. Join us."
**When to use:** Unaware (identity-first) and Most Aware (community reinforcement). C4 angle campaigns.
**Common mistake:** Claiming unity without earning it. You must actually share the identity — faking belonging is instantly detected.

## Common Stacking Combinations

| Combo | How it works | Example |
|---|---|---|
| P4 + P3 | Authority delivers the claim, social proof confirms it | "After 200 brands, here's the #1 pattern. 12k strategists agree." |
| P1 + P2 | Free value creates reciprocity, then micro-commitment deepens it | "Free framework → take the quiz → see your results → join" |
| P6 + P3 | Scarcity creates urgency, social proof removes doubt | "6 spots left. 2,400 already inside." |
| P7 + P5 | Unity builds tribe, liking builds connection to the messenger | "We don't run ads without hypotheses. I learned that the hard way." |
