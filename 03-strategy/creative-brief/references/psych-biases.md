# Psychological Biases for Advertising

Cognitive biases are mental shortcuts the brain uses to make fast decisions. In advertising, they're levers — when you know which bias your prospect is susceptible to in a given moment, you can structure the ad to work WITH their brain, not against it.

**Usage rule:** Pick one dominant bias per creative. Stacking biases dilutes impact and feels manipulative.

---

## The 15 Key Biases

### B01 — Loss Aversion
**Definition:** Losing something feels ~2x more painful than gaining the same thing feels good (Kahneman & Tversky).
**How it works in ads:** Frame the cost of inaction, not just the benefit of action. Show what they'll miss, lose, or waste.
**Example headline:** "Every day without this system, you're losing €47 in wasted ad spend."
**Best angle pairing:** C1 (Problem) — P4 Projected Pain
**Best awareness stage:** Problem Aware, Product Aware

---

### B02 — Social Proof
**Definition:** When uncertain, people look at what others do to decide what's correct.
**How it works in ads:** Show volume (numbers), similarity (people like them), or authority (experts endorsing).
**Example headline:** "12,400 media buyers switched to this workflow last quarter."
**Best angle pairing:** C2 (Result) — R3 Third-Party Testimonial
**Best awareness stage:** Solution Aware, Product Aware

---

### B03 — Anchoring
**Definition:** The first number or reference point heard becomes the benchmark for all subsequent judgments.
**How it works in ads:** Present a high price, high effort, or high time investment first — then reveal your solution as dramatically lower.
**Example headline:** "Agencies charge €3,000/month for what this does in 15 minutes."
**Best angle pairing:** C2 (Result) — R1 Numbers and Metrics
**Best awareness stage:** Solution Aware, Product Aware

---

### B04 — Scarcity
**Definition:** Limited availability increases perceived value. What's rare feels more desirable.
**How it works in ads:** Genuine limits on time, stock, or access. Must be real — fake scarcity destroys trust.
**Example headline:** "6 spots left for the April cohort. Next opening: September."
**Best angle pairing:** C2 (Result) + C1 (Problem — cost of waiting)
**Best awareness stage:** Product Aware, Most Aware

---

### B05 — Bandwagon Effect
**Definition:** People adopt behaviors they see others adopting — especially people in their identity group.
**How it works in ads:** Show momentum. "Everyone is moving to X" creates pressure to not be left behind.
**Example headline:** "The framework 300+ e-commerce brands adopted this year."
**Best angle pairing:** C4 (Identity) — I2 Community
**Best awareness stage:** Solution Aware, Product Aware

---

### B06 — Authority Bias
**Definition:** People trust and follow the opinions of perceived experts or credentialed figures.
**How it works in ads:** Lead with credentials, certifications, media mentions, or experience markers before the argument.
**Example headline:** "After managing €12M in ad spend, here's the one thing I'd change."
**Best angle pairing:** C3 (Education) — E1 Revelation
**Best awareness stage:** Solution Aware, Product Aware

---

### B07 — Confirmation Bias
**Definition:** People seek and favor information that confirms what they already believe.
**How it works in ads:** Start by validating a belief the prospect holds — then redirect it toward your solution.
**Example headline:** "You're right — most ads fail. Here's why yours don't have to."
**Best angle pairing:** C3 (Education) — E3 Myth-Busting
**Best awareness stage:** Problem Aware, Solution Aware

---

### B08 — Framing Effect
**Definition:** The same information presented differently leads to different decisions. "90% success" vs "10% failure."
**How it works in ads:** Frame results positively for aspiration hooks, frame risks negatively for pain hooks.
**Example headline:** "9 out of 10 brands that use creative testing scale past €100k/month." (vs "1 in 10 fail")
**Best angle pairing:** C2 (Result) — R1 Numbers
**Best awareness stage:** All levels — depends on frame direction

---

### B09 — Endowment Effect
**Definition:** People overvalue things they already possess or feel ownership over.
**How it works in ads:** Give them a taste first — free trial, preview, sample. Once they "own" it mentally, losing it feels painful.
**Example headline:** "Try the full system free for 14 days. You won't want to go back."
**Best angle pairing:** C2 (Result) — R2 Lifestyle Transformation
**Best awareness stage:** Product Aware, Most Aware

---

### B10 — Status Quo Bias
**Definition:** People prefer the current state of affairs. Change requires more energy than staying put.
**How it works in ads:** Make the cost of NOT changing more vivid than the effort of changing. "If nothing changes, nothing changes."
**Example headline:** "Same strategy, same results. How long can you afford that?"
**Best angle pairing:** C1 (Problem) — P4 Projected Pain
**Best awareness stage:** Problem Aware

---

### B11 — Curiosity Gap
**Definition:** An incomplete piece of information creates cognitive tension that demands resolution.
**How it works in ads:** Open a loop in the hook that can only be closed by watching/reading further.
**Example headline:** "The one metric 90% of media buyers ignore — that predicts scale."
**Best angle pairing:** C3 (Education) — E1 Revelation
**Best awareness stage:** Unaware, Problem Aware

---

### B12 — Reciprocity
**Definition:** When someone gives you something, you feel obligated to give back.
**How it works in ads:** Lead with genuine value — a tip, a framework, a free tool. The prospect feels they "owe" you attention.
**Example headline:** "Free: the exact hook formula we use for €50k/month accounts."
**Best angle pairing:** C3 (Education) — E4 How-To
**Best awareness stage:** Problem Aware, Solution Aware

---

### B13 — FOMO (Fear of Missing Out)
**Definition:** Anxiety triggered by the possibility that others are having an experience you're excluded from.
**How it works in ads:** Show peers already benefiting. Make the prospect feel like they're falling behind.
**Example headline:** "While you're still testing manually, your competitors automated last month."
**Best angle pairing:** C1 (Problem) — P3 Social Pain
**Best awareness stage:** Solution Aware, Product Aware

---

### B14 — Halo Effect
**Definition:** One positive trait (attractiveness, success, brand recognition) colors perception of everything else.
**How it works in ads:** Associate the product with a respected figure, brand, or outcome. The positive glow transfers.
**Example headline:** "The tool recommended by [known figure] for creative testing."
**Best angle pairing:** C4 (Identity) — I4 Status
**Best awareness stage:** Solution Aware, Product Aware

---

### B15 — Sunk Cost Fallacy
**Definition:** People continue investing in something because of what they've already put in — even when it's not working.
**How it works in ads:** Name the sunk cost, then give them permission to pivot. "You've already spent X — don't waste it."
**Example headline:** "You've already spent €10k on ads. Don't let bad creatives waste the next €10k."
**Best angle pairing:** C1 (Problem) — P2 Emotional Pain
**Best awareness stage:** Problem Aware, Solution Aware
